Modern America

The Progressive Era, America's Age of Reform

Picture of the Second Industrial Revolution factories (1870 – 1914)

America was booming economicly and socially with the Second Industrial Revolution during the start of the 1900s. While this brought wealth and growth, it also led to a host of issues such as economic inequality, poor working conditions, political corruption, and societal unrest. In response, a diverse movement of reformers arose, sharing the common goal of making American society better for all - the Progressives.

During the Progressive Era there was a belief that the government had a role to play in addressing societal issues and curbing the power of big corporations, often called "trusts". Reformers worked to introduce legislation and amend the Constitution to usher in political changes.

A political cartoon of Teddy Roosevelt, a progressive era president busting big corporation trusts (1901 – 1909)

Women rights demonstrators holding a rally in New York (1916)

Significant amendments during this time included the 16th Amendment, allowing the federal government to tax income, and the 19th Amendment, granting women the right to vote. The era also saw the implementation of the direct election of senators through the 17th Amendment.

Progressives dealt with societal issues as well, advocating for better education, improved public health, conservation of natural resources, and rights for workers, including the end of child labor.

Theodore Roosevelt (1901 – 1909)

At the political level, Presidents such as Theodore Roosevelt and Woodrow Wilson played significant roles in pushing for progressive policies. Roosevelt's "Square Deal" and Wilson's "New Freedom" aimed to curb corporate power, protect workers' rights, and improve government accountability.

The Roaring Twenties

Indian Removal Act - 1830

The 1830s saw the enactment of the Indian Removal Act under President Andrew Jackson, resulting in the removal of thousands of Native Americans from their lands, primarily from the Cherokee, Creek, Choctaw, Chickasaw, and Seminole tribes, in what became known as the Trail of Tears. The federal government believed that by removing Native American tribes and relocating them to remote territories, they could mitigate conflicts and make way for the settlement of white Americans.

The Oregon Trail was a historic overland migration route used by pioneers and settlers to travel from the eastern United States to the Oregon Territory in the Pacific Northwest. It epitomizes the period of Manifest Destiny, a belief in the divine sanction for American expansion and a lure of fertile lands, economic opportunities, and the promise of a better life. Pioneers traveled in wagons, typically composed of family groups or individuals. They faced grueling conditions, enduring months of travel, with limited supplies and basic makeshift accommodations.

Mexican-American War - 1846-1848

The Mexican-American War resulted in the U.S. gaining significant territory in the West, including California and New Mexico. The war was caused by territorial disputes between the United States and Mexico over the boundaries of Texas, which had declared independence from Mexico in 1836 and later joined the United States in 1845. The United States claimed that the border was the Rio Grande, while Mexico insisted it was the Nueces River. Additionally, the United States sought to expand its territorial holdings further west, including California and New Mexico. The war would end with an American victory and the war's conclusion with the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, increasing Americas land.

The discovery of gold in California in 1848 led to the Gold Rush, attracting hundreds of thousands of prospectors to the West and further accelerating the westward migration and expansion. The Gold Rush had a profound impact on migration patterns and the settlement of the American West. The rapid influx of prospectors accelerated the westward expansion of the United States, as people from various backgrounds and regions sought their fortunes in the gold fields.
