South Carolina

Founded in 1670

Panting of Charleston, South Carolina.

Originally, North Carolina and South Carolina were one colony (Carolina) before being split up into two colonies in 1712. In 1670, the first settlers, led by Sir John Colleton, settled in what would become the city of Charleston. Because of its southward position, the colony would become a major hub for using slavery to farm cash crops, such as rice and indigo.

Georgia

Founded in 1732

A painting of historic Savannah, the oldest city in Georgia.

Georgia was the last of the Thirteen Colonies to be established. Georgia was established by James Oglethrope in 1732. He wanted Georgia to serve as a refuge for those who are poor and in debt, as well as act as a buffer between English colonies and Spanish Colonies. The first settlers came to Georgia in 1733 and established the town of Savannah.

North Carolina

Founded in 1663

A recreation of a building in Bath, the oldest town in North Carolina.

In the 1650's, travelers from Virginia started venturing southward and established settlements below Virginia's southern border. Then in 1663, King Charles II granted a charter to establish a royal colony for lands below Virginia. This colony would be called Carolina (and eventually broken up into North and South Carolina). Like Virginia, North Carolina relied heavely on farming tobacco.

Virginia

Founded in 1607

Recreation of the Jamestown settlement in the Colony of Virginia.

Virginia was one of the first English colonies to be established in North America. In 1607, the Virginia Company established the settlement of Jamestown along the James River. The unskilled settlers faced numerous challenges, including conflicts with Native American tribes and harsh living conditions. However, the colony eventually flourished under the leadership of John Smith and John Rolfe. In 1619, Virginia introduced representative government to America with the establishment of the House of Burgesses, the first legislative house in the 13 colonies. However, the colony also marks the beginning of slavery in North America, with the arrival of the first African slaves in 1619.

Maryland

Founded in 1633

Recration of some buildings in St. Mary's City.

Maryland was established in 1633 by Cecilius Calvert, also known as Lord Baltimore. The colony was meant to serve as a refuge for Catholics fleeing persecution from England. This made the colony unique, as it was one of the first colonies to try to legislate religious toleration. The first settlers arrived in 1634 and established the town of St. Mary's City. Like many colonies nearby, it developed a tobacco-farming industry and would go on to use slave labor.

Delaware

Founded in 1638

Old Swedes Church in Delaware, one of the oldest churches in America, built by the first Swedish settlers.

In 1638, the Swedes established New Sweden, with Fort Christina as the capital. The Dutch captured New Sweden in 1655, bringing Delaware under Dutch control. In 1664, the English seized Delaware during the Second Anglo-Dutch War, and it became part of New York. In 1682, Delaware was granted to William Penn and became part of Pennsylvania, but eventually separated and declared independence in 1776, becoming the first state to ratify the U.S. Constitution in 1787.

New Jersey

Founded in 1664

A photo of a Quaker meeting house in Burlingtion, New Jersey created in 1678.

In the early 1600s, Dutch explorers established trading posts along the Hudson River. In 1664, the English captured the Dutch settlement of New Amsterdam, renaming it New York, and also gained control of the land that would become New Jersey. In 1665, the Duke of York granted the land between the Hudson and Delaware Rivers to two noblemen, Lord John Berkeley and Sir George Carteret, who established the proprietary colony of New Jersey. The colony attracted settlers from various backgrounds, including English, Dutch, and Swedish, and developed a diverse economy based on agriculture, trade, and commerce. Interestingly, New Jersey was divided into East Jersey and West Jersey in 1674, each having its own government. However, the colony would eventually reunite as a single royal colony in 1702.

Pennsylvania

Founded in 1682

A painting of historic Philadelphia, a major city in Pennsylvania's early history.

Pennsylvania was founded by William Penn in the late 17th century after receiving a charter from King Charles II in 1681. Penn arrived in 1682 and established Philadelphia as the capital. He implemented a government based on Quaker principles, promoting religious freedom and peaceful relations with Native Americans. Pennsylvania attracted settlers from various European countries, offering generous land policies and religious tolerance. Under Penn's leadership, the colony prospered through agriculture, trade, and industry. Pennsylvania played a significant role in the American Revolution and became one of the original thirteen states to ratify the U.S. Constitution.

New York

Founded in 1624

A painting of The Castello Plan city map of Amsterdam, located in New York or New Amsterdam under Dutch rule.

In 1624, the Dutch established the first permanent European settlement called New Amsterdam on the southern tip of Manhattan Island. In 1664, the English seized control of New Amsterdam, renaming it New York in honor of the Duke of York. The English continued to develop the colony, attracting diverse settlers and establishing a proprietary government.

Connecticut

Founded in 1636

Recration of the Parker House in Old Saybrook, Connecticut. Believed to be one of the first buildings built in Connecticut.

Connecticut was founded in the 17th century as a result of the expansion of English colonies in New England. In 1633, English settlers from the Massachusetts Bay Colony established a trading post at Windsor, marking the beginning of Connecticut. In 1636, Reverend Thomas Hooker and his congregation founded the town of Hartford, followed by other settlements such as Windsor and Wethersfield. In 1639, the settlers drafted the Fundamental Orders, often considered the first written constitution in North America, which established a democratic government for Connecticut. The colony attracted Puritan settlers seeking religious freedom and a more democratic society.

Rhode Island

Founded in 1636

Panting of Newport Harbor, a famous site in Rhode Island.

Rhode Island was founded in the 17th century as a haven for religious freedom. In 1636, Roger Williams, a Puritan minister who was expelled from the Massachusetts Bay Colony due to his dissenting views, established a settlement called Providence. Williams advocated for the separation of church and state and welcomed people of diverse religious beliefs to the colony. In 1644, the colony received a royal charter from King Charles II, formally recognizing its existence. In 1647, Rhode Island passed the first law in the American colonies guaranteeing religious freedom. The colony grew as more settlers seeking religious tolerance and economic opportunities flocked to Rhode Island. In 1663, Rhode Island obtained a new royal charter, which granted it self-governance and expanded religious freedoms, laying the foundation for its future as a democratic state.

Massachusetts

Founded in 1630

Recration of a building in Plymouth, the site of the first Pilgrim settlement, founded in 1620.

Massachusetts was founded in the early 17th century by English Puritans seeking religious freedom. In 1620, the Pilgrims arrived on the Mayflower and established the Plymouth Colony. In 1629, the Massachusetts Bay Company, led by John Winthrop, obtained a royal charter to create a larger settlement. In 1630, a fleet of Puritan settlers arrived, establishing the Massachusetts Bay Colony with Boston as its capital. The colony grew rapidly as more Puritans migrated, establishing towns and villages throughout the region. The Puritans created a theocratic government, with the church playing a central role in the colony's affairs. In 1691, Massachusetts underwent a political transformation when the Plymouth Colony merged with the Massachusetts Bay Colony to form the Province of Massachusetts Bay, establishing a royal charter and increasing royal control over the colony.

New Hampshire

Founded in 1623

Panting of Portsmouth, New Hampshire from across the Piscataqua River.

New Hampshire was founded in the early 17th century as a result of territorial disputes between the English and the French. In 1623, Captain John Mason received a land grant from the Council for New England and established a fishing village called New Hampshire. The settlement faced numerous challenges, including conflicts with Native American tribes and economic struggles. In 1638, Massachusetts began exerting control over New Hampshire, incorporating it as a part of Massachusetts. However, in 1679, New Hampshire was separated from Massachusetts and became a royal colony. The colony continued to grow through fishing, trade, and agriculture, attracting settlers seeking economic opportunities.
